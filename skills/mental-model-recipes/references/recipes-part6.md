## Category 8: Relationships & Negotiation

*When the problem involves other people's perspectives, incentives, and power.*

---

### Recipe 33: The Negotiation Mapper

**Use when:** You're entering a negotiation — for a deal, a salary, a partnership, a conflict resolution — and you need to understand the full landscape before you begin. The stakes are high enough that going in without a complete map would be costly.

**When NOT to use:** When the negotiation is routine and the parameters are well-known. When you have significantly more leverage and a simple approach will work. Simpler heuristic: "What do I want, what's the minimum I'll accept, and what's my best alternative if this fails?"

**Step 1:** Perspective Simulation via Full Role Entry, through the lens of Asymmetric Information.
"Become the other party. What do they know that I don't? What do they WANT — not their stated position but their underlying interests? What are they afraid of? What's their best alternative if this negotiation fails? If their BATNA is strong, my leverage is weak regardless of my arguments."

**Step 2:** Systems Thinking via Leverage Point Analysis, through the lens of Principal-Agent Problem.
"Map the full incentive landscape. Who are the decision-makers, and what are their personal incentives (which may differ from their organization's interests)? Where are the leverage points — the specific factors that would move their position most? Are there third parties whose actions could change the landscape entirely?"

**Step 3:** Counterfactual Analysis via Substitution Test, through the lens of Zero-Sum vs. Positive-Sum.
"Is this negotiation actually zero-sum (your gain is my loss), or can we create additional value? What would change if we substituted a different deal structure — different terms, different timeline, different bundling? Often the most effective move isn't bargaining harder within the current frame but proposing a different frame entirely."

**Step 4:** Dialectical Synthesis via Both/And Reframe, through the lens of Optionality.
"What's the synthesis that gives both parties more than they expected — not a compromise where both get less, but a creative restructuring where both get more? Design at least three different deal structures that satisfy both parties' core interests through different mechanisms."

**Abandon when surprised:** If Full Role Entry reveals that the other party's BATNA is significantly stronger than you assumed — or that they have information you lack — pause. Improve your alternative before negotiating from weakness.

**The prompt:**
"I'm entering a negotiation for [X]. First, become the other party: what do they actually want (not their stated position), what are they afraid of, and what's their best alternative if this fails? Second, map the full incentive landscape — who decides, what are their personal incentives, and where are the leverage points? Third, is this zero-sum or can we create value? What different deal structures would change the dynamic? Fourth, what synthesis gives both parties MORE than expected — design three creative structures that satisfy both sides' core interests."

**What makes this recipe unique:** Most negotiation prep focuses on your position and your arguments. This recipe starts with the OTHER party's internal landscape. Full Role Entry (understanding their true interests) + Leverage Point Analysis (mapping incentives and power) + Substitution Test (reframing the deal structure) + Both/And Reframe (creating mutual value) turns adversarial negotiation into value creation while keeping you realistic about power dynamics.

### Recipe 34: The Conflict Resolver

**Use when:** Two parties (or two parts of yourself) are locked in opposition. Neither side is willing to move, and the conflict is consuming energy. Compromise has been attempted and rejected — both sides feel they'd lose too much.

**When NOT to use:** When one side is clearly right and the other clearly wrong. When the conflict is serving a productive function (healthy creative tension). Simpler heuristic: "Is this a problem to solve or a tension to manage?"

**Step 1:** Perspective Simulation via Ideological Turing Test, through the lens of Revealed Preferences.
"State each side's position so accurately that they would endorse your summary. Then check: does each side's BEHAVIOR match their stated position? Often conflicts persist because the stated disagreement masks a deeper one — visible in what people DO, not what they SAY."

**Step 2:** First Principles via Zeroth Principle, through the lens of Frame Control.
"Question the frame of the conflict itself. Both sides have accepted a shared frame — 'we disagree about X.' But what if the frame is wrong? What if they actually agree about X and disagree about Y, which hasn't been named?"

**Step 3:** Abductive Reasoning via Surprising Absence Detection, through the lens of Mimetic Desire.
"What needs are NOT being expressed in this conflict? Often conflicts persist because the real issue — status, respect, autonomy, fear — can't be said directly. What's conspicuously absent from both sides' stated concerns? Also: are the parties in conflict because they genuinely disagree, or because they're competing for the same thing (mimetic rivalry)?"

**Step 4:** Dialectical Synthesis via Determinate Negation, through the lens of Paradox Integration.
"Each side is specifically wrong about something — not generically wrong. Name the specific error in each position. Let those specific errors generate their own corrections. The resolution emerges from transcending the shared limitation that created the conflict."

**Abandon when surprised:** If the Zeroth Principle in Step 2 reveals that both sides actually agree on the fundamental issue and are fighting about implementation, the conflict is dramatically simpler than it appeared. Redirect to implementation specifics.

**The prompt:**
"[Party A] and [Party B] are locked in conflict over [X]. First, state each side's position accurately enough they'd endorse it, then check if their behavior matches their stated position. Second, question the frame: are they actually disagreeing about what they think they're disagreeing about? Third, what's NOT being said — what needs (status, respect, autonomy, fear) are hiding beneath the stated positions? Is this genuine disagreement or mimetic rivalry? Fourth, name what each side is specifically wrong about and let those errors generate a resolution that transcends both positions."

**What makes this recipe unique:** It treats conflicts as mislabeled rather than intractable. Ideological Turing Test (testing understanding) + Zeroth Principle (questioning the frame) + Surprising Absence Detection (finding hidden needs) + Determinate Negation (using each side's error to generate resolution) is designed for the specific failure mode where compromise has already failed — because the real conflict was never about what both sides claimed.

### Recipe 35: The Stakeholder Aligner

**Use when:** Multiple parties need to agree but have different priorities, incentives, and information. No one is wrong — they're just optimizing for different things. The project can't move forward without alignment, and forcing a decision would undermine buy-in.

**When NOT to use:** When a single decision-maker can and should decide unilaterally. When the alignment overhead exceeds the value of broad buy-in. Simpler heuristic: "Who has the authority and the best information? Let them decide."

**Step 1:** Perspective Simulation via Stakeholder Voice Simulation, through the lens of Principal-Agent Problem.
"Simulate each stakeholder. What are they optimizing for? What do they fear? What information do they have that others don't? What would they need to hear — and from whom — to feel safe agreeing?"

**Step 2:** Systems Thinking via Leverage Point Analysis, through the lens of Network Effects.
"Find the keystone stakeholder — the person whose agreement would make others more likely to agree. Map the influence network and identify the optimal sequence of conversations."

**Step 3:** Abductive Reasoning via Pattern Completion, through the lens of Venn Diagram Analysis.
"Where do all stakeholders' interests overlap? This is the zone of possible agreement. What proposal lives in that overlap? And what would EXPAND the overlap — what new information, what restructured deal, what additional value could enlarge the zone?"

**Step 4:** Dialectical Synthesis via Constructive Disagreement Protocol, through the lens of Commitment and Consistency Bias.
"Each stakeholder must: state their strongest concern, name what's genuinely valid in the others' concerns, and propose a solution that addresses at least two other stakeholders' core worries. The synthesis from structured exchange is typically more creative than any pre-designed proposal."

**Abandon when surprised:** If Stakeholder Voice Simulation reveals that one stakeholder has fundamental veto power AND a non-negotiable position contradicting others, alignment may be impossible through consensus. You need executive decision-making, not alignment.

**The prompt:**
"I need alignment from [stakeholders] on [X], and they have different priorities. First, simulate each stakeholder: what are they optimizing for, what do they fear, what information do they uniquely have, and what would they need to hear to feel safe agreeing? Second, find the keystone stakeholder: whose agreement makes others more likely to agree? Map the optimal sequence. Third, where do all interests overlap? What proposal lives there, and what could expand it? Fourth, have each stakeholder name what's valid in others' concerns and propose a solution that addresses at least two others' worries."

**What makes this recipe unique:** Alignment isn't agreement — it's finding the structure where everyone can move forward. Stakeholder Voice Simulation (mapping the landscape) + Leverage Point Analysis (finding the keystone) + Pattern Completion (locating overlap) + Constructive Disagreement Protocol (structured synthesis) is designed for multi-party coordination where each party is rational but optimizing for different outcomes.

## Category 9: Personal Development & Identity

*When the constraint isn't intellectual but developmental — who you are is limiting what you can do.*

---

### Recipe 36: The Identity Audit

**Use when:** You sense that an outdated identity is constraining your growth. You know who you WERE and it was useful, but it no longer fits who you need to BECOME. The old identity persists as a ghost, shaping decisions you can feel but can't fully articulate.

**When NOT to use:** When the identity is genuinely functional and the discomfort is about external pressure to change. When the issue is a skill gap rather than an identity constraint. Simpler heuristic: "Do I need to become someone different, or learn something new as who I already am?"

**Step 1:** First Principles via Assumption Mapping, through the lens of Revealed Preferences.
"List every belief about who you are — 'I'm a [role], I value [values], I'm the person who [behaviors].' Check each against your behavior over the last 6 months. Where do stated identity beliefs diverge from revealed preferences? The gaps are the cracks in the old identity."

**Step 2:** Falsification via Crucial Experiment, through the lens of Sunk Cost Awareness.
"For the identity elements that no longer match your behavior, ask: what evidence would prove this identity element is wrong for who I am now? Design a clean test. Can you try being someone different for a defined period and observe what happens?"

**Step 3:** Abductive Reasoning via Pattern Completion, through the lens of J-Curve.
"If the old identity is shedding, what's the new one trying to emerge? What patterns in your recent behavior, interests, and energy suggest the shape of who you're becoming? Note: identity transitions often follow a J-curve — things get worse before they get better. The confusion may be the dip, not the destination."

**Step 4:** Dialectical Synthesis via Paradox Integration, through the lens of Adaptive Valley.
"The old identity and the emerging one aren't necessarily enemies. What's worth preserving from who you were? What must be let go? What identity holds both — honoring your past while making space for your future? Crossing the adaptive valley requires temporarily being 'neither' — and that discomfort is productive."

**Abandon when surprised:** If Assumption Mapping in Step 1 reveals that your stated identity and revealed preferences are ALREADY aligned to the new identity — you've been living as the new person while still calling yourself the old one — the audit is complete. Acknowledge who you already are.

**The prompt:**
"I sense an outdated identity is holding me back. First, map every belief about who I am and check each against my actual behavior over the last 6 months — where do stated identity and revealed preferences diverge? Second, for each outdated element, design a clean test: what would prove it's wrong for who I am now, and can I try being different for a defined period? Third, what patterns in my recent behavior suggest the shape of who I'm becoming — and am I in the J-curve dip? Fourth, what's worth preserving from the old identity, what must go, and what identity holds both while making space for growth?"

**What makes this recipe unique:** Most development recipes work on skills or thinking. This one works on IDENTITY — the deepest constraint on growth. Assumption Mapping against Revealed Preferences (finding cracks) + Crucial Experiment (testing outdated elements) + Pattern Completion (detecting emerging identity) + Paradox Integration (honoring both old and new) is designed for the developmental challenge of outgrowing who you were.

### Recipe 37: The Growth Edge Finder

**Use when:** You've mastered your current level and feel the pull toward something you can't yet articulate. You're competent but not growing. The plateau isn't about skill — it's about the limit of your current developmental stage.

**When NOT to use:** When you're still consolidating a recent growth spurt. When the plateau is due to external constraints. Simpler heuristic: "Am I being challenged enough by my environment, or is the stagnation internal?"

**Step 1:** Perspective Simulation via Opposite Expertise Test, through the lens of Dunning-Kruger Effect.
"Find someone who operates at the next level — not just more skilled but developmentally more complex. What do they see that you can't? Not what do they KNOW (skill gap) but how do they PERCEIVE (developmental gap). The difference between skill and perception is the growth edge."

**Step 2:** Abductive Reasoning via Anomaly Hunting, through the lens of Local Optimum vs. Global Optimum.
"What keeps surprising you? What outcomes make no sense from your current framework? Recurring surprises are signals that your mental model is incomplete — and their shape reveals what you're missing. These anomalies cluster at your growth edge."

**Step 3:** Counterfactual Analysis via Thought Experiment, through the lens of Adaptive Valley.
"Imagine you could ALREADY think at the next level. What would you see differently? What decisions would you make differently? What would you stop doing? The gap between current and next-level perception IS the growth edge. Crossing it requires temporarily getting worse."

**Step 4:** Falsification via Crucial Experiment, through the lens of Commitment and Consistency Bias.
"Design a small experiment that requires you to operate AT your growth edge — not within your competence zone and not so far beyond it that you'll fail immediately. Your resistance to the experiment reveals what the growth edge is asking you to let go of."

**Abandon when surprised:** If Anomaly Hunting reveals that your surprises cluster in a specific domain you've been avoiding — the area you're most reluctant to examine — that's almost certainly where the growth edge lives.

**The prompt:**
"I've mastered my current level but I'm not growing. First, find someone who operates at the next developmental level — what do they PERCEIVE that I can't, beyond just knowing more? Second, what keeps surprising me? Where does my framework fail to predict outcomes? Third, imagine I could already think at the next level — what would I see, decide, and stop doing differently? Fourth, design a small experiment at the edge of my competence that makes me uncomfortable — and notice what I'm resistant to letting go of."

**What makes this recipe unique:** It distinguishes between skill growth (learning more at the same level) and developmental growth (ascending to a new level of complexity). Opposite Expertise Test (seeing the next level) + Anomaly Hunting (finding where your framework breaks) + Thought Experiment (imagining next-level perception) + Crucial Experiment (designing a growth-edge challenge) is designed for transitioning from "competent at this level" to "ready for the next level."

### Recipe 38: The Values Clarifier

**Use when:** Your stated values and your revealed preferences are in conflict, and the dissonance is creating problems — burnout, guilt, chronic dissatisfaction, or repeated hollow decisions. You need to figure out what you actually value, not what you think you should value.

**When NOT to use:** When your values are clear and the conflict is purely practical. When the dissonance is temporary and situational. Simpler heuristic: "Is this a values problem or a strategy problem?"

**Step 1:** First Principles via Assumption Mapping, through the lens of Revealed Preferences.
"List your stated values. Then look at your calendar, bank statements, and emotional reactions over the last month. These reveal your ACTUAL values. Map the gaps. Where you spend time and money is what you value; where you feel anger, joy, or guilt reveals your real hierarchy."

**Step 2:** Abductive Reasoning via Diagnostic Reasoning, through the lens of Mimetic Desire.
"For each gap between stated and revealed values: Am I holding this stated value because I genuinely chose it — or because my culture, family, or peer group transmitted it? Which of your stated values would survive if you were the last person on earth?"

**Step 3:** Counterfactual Analysis via Regret Minimization, through the lens of Peak-End Rule.
"Look at your life's peak moments — the times of deepest satisfaction and deepest regret. What values were being honored at the peaks? What values were being violated at the low points? Your peak-end pattern reveals your actual value hierarchy more reliably than any self-report."

**Step 4:** Dialectical Synthesis via Paradox Integration, through the lens of Commitment and Consistency Bias.
"Some value conflicts are genuine paradoxes — you truly value both security and freedom, both achievement and presence. Don't force a resolution. Ask: is there a way of living that HOLDS both without sacrificing either? The resolution is often not choosing between values but finding the level at which they stop contradicting."

**Abandon when surprised:** If Step 1 reveals a glaring gap — something you claim to value highly that receives zero time, energy, or resources — name it without judgment. Either recommit concretely or release it honestly.

**The prompt:**
"My stated values and actual behavior are in conflict and it's creating [burnout/guilt/dissatisfaction]. First, list my stated values and compare them to where I actually spend time and money — where are the gaps? Second, for each gap, is this value genuinely mine or imported from culture, family, or peers? Which values would survive if I were the last person on earth? Third, look at my life's peak moments of satisfaction and regret — what values were being honored or violated? Fourth, for genuine value conflicts, is there a way of living that holds both without sacrifice — at what level do they stop contradicting?"

**What makes this recipe unique:** It uses behavior as data to reveal values, bypassing self-deception. Assumption Mapping against Revealed Preferences (mapping gaps) + Mimetic Desire analysis (testing which values are genuinely yours) + Peak-End Regret analysis (extracting actual hierarchy) + Paradox Integration (holding genuine tensions) reads values from behavior, not from self-report.

## Category 10: Systems & Organizational

*When the problem lives at the system level — no individual action can solve it.*

---

### Recipe 39: The Incentive Auditor

**Use when:** People are behaving in ways that seem irrational or counterproductive, but they're not stupid. When smart people consistently do dumb things, the problem isn't the people — it's the incentive structure.

**When NOT to use:** When the behavior really is individual incompetence. When the incentives are known and the problem is enforcement. Simpler heuristic: "Are people doing what the system rewards them to do?"

**Step 1:** Abductive Reasoning via Diagnostic Reasoning, through the lens of Revealed Preferences.
"The behavior you're seeing IS the diagnosis. People are doing what makes sense given the incentives they face. What incentive structure would make this behavior RATIONAL? Work backward from the behavior to the hidden reward structure."

**Step 2:** Systems Thinking via Causal Loop Diagramming, through the lens of Goodhart's Law.
"Map the full incentive loop: what metrics are being measured? What behaviors are those metrics rewarding? Are the metrics still measuring what they were designed to measure — or have they become targets that people game?"

**Step 3:** Counterfactual Analysis via Substitution Test, through the lens of Cobra Effect.
"If you replaced the current incentive structure with an alternative, would the behavior change? More importantly: would the NEW incentive create its OWN perverse behaviors? Every incentive redesign risks creating new unintended consequences. Simulate the response before implementing."

**Step 4:** Falsification via Crucial Experiment, through the lens of Skin in the Game.
"Can you test the new incentive structure in a contained environment before rolling it out? Design a pilot that reveals whether the redesigned incentives produce the intended behavior change. And check: do the people who designed the incentive structure bear any consequences from its outcomes?"

**Abandon when surprised:** If Step 1 reveals that the behavior is perfectly rational given the incentives — AND the incentives are themselves rational given the broader system — you may be looking at an emergent property, not a design flaw.

**The prompt:**
"People in [system/organization] keep doing [behavior] that seems irrational. First, work backward from the behavior: what incentive structure makes this rational? What is it being rewarded with? Second, map the full incentive loop: what metrics are being measured, what behaviors are they actually rewarding, and have the metrics become gaming targets? Third, if I redesign the incentives, would the new structure create its own perverse behaviors? Simulate the response. Fourth, can I test the redesign in a contained pilot — and do the incentive designers themselves bear consequences from outcomes?"

**What makes this recipe unique:** It treats "irrational" behavior as rational-given-incentives. Diagnostic Reasoning from Revealed Preferences (reading incentives from behavior) + Causal Loop Diagramming with Goodhart's Law (mapping broken feedback) + Substitution Test with Cobra Effect (avoiding perverse redesign) + Crucial Experiment with Skin in the Game (testing before deploying) is designed for the pattern where the system itself causes the behavior everyone tries to fix individually.

### Recipe 40: The Organizational Immune System Detector

**Use when:** Good ideas keep dying inside the organization. Innovation initiatives launch with enthusiasm and fizzle within months. New hires with fresh perspectives gradually conform. Something is systematically killing change — and it's not any individual's fault.

**When NOT to use:** When the ideas that keep dying actually deserve to die. When the organization's stability is a genuine strength that innovation threatens unnecessarily. Simpler heuristic: "Are the ideas actually good, or is the organization right to reject them?"

**Step 1:** Systems Thinking via Archetypes Recognition, through the lens of Homeostasis.
"Identify which system archetype is operating. 'Shifting the Burden' (short-term fixes undermining fundamental solution)? 'Limits to Growth' (hidden constraint capping progress)? 'Success to the Successful' (existing winners starving innovation)? The organization isn't malicious — it's maintaining homeostasis. The immune system is doing its job: protecting the organism from foreign bodies. Unfortunately, the innovation IS the foreign body."

**Step 2:** Abductive Reasoning via Abductive Cascade, through the lens of Barriers to Exit.
"Why does innovation die? Go deeper with each level: 'Ideas die because they lose executive sponsorship' → 'They lose sponsorship because short-term results don't materialize' → 'Short-term results don't materialize because innovation requires patient capital' → 'Patient capital doesn't exist because the evaluation system is quarterly' → 'The evaluation system is quarterly because Wall Street rewards quarterly earnings.' Each level reveals a deeper structural cause."

**Step 3:** Counterfactual Analysis via Absence Counterfactual, through the lens of Chesterton's Fence.
"What would happen if the organizational immune system were turned OFF? You'd probably get chaos. The immune system exists for a reason: it prevents bad ideas from consuming resources, maintains operational consistency, preserves institutional knowledge. The goal isn't to destroy the immune system — it's to make it smarter. It needs to distinguish between threats and nutrients."

**Step 4:** Analogical Reasoning via Biomimicry, through the lens of Antifragility.
"How do biological immune systems handle this? They learn. They distinguish between self and non-self. They have regulated responses that can tolerate beneficial foreign substances (like food in the gut). What's the organizational equivalent of immune tolerance — the mechanism that would allow accepting beneficial change while rejecting genuinely harmful disruption?"

**Step 5:** Falsification via Stress Testing, through the lens of Leverage Points (Meadows).
"What's the minimum intervention that would change the immune system's behavior without destroying it? Typically: changing what information flows to whom (Meadows' leverage point #7), changing the rules of what gets funded (#8), or changing the goals from 'maintain current performance' to 'maintain current AND build future capability' (#10). Test the minimum intervention first."

**Abandon when surprised:** If the Abductive Cascade reveals that the organization's immune system was deliberately designed by leadership to prevent exactly the type of change you're pursuing, the problem isn't organizational — it's a leadership alignment problem. You need a different conversation.

**The prompt:**
"Good ideas keep dying in [organization]. First, which system archetype is operating — Shifting the Burden, Limits to Growth, or Success to the Successful? The organization is maintaining homeostasis, not being malicious. Second, trace why innovation dies through deepening levels of cause — go five levels deep. Third, what would happen if the innovation-killing mechanisms were turned off entirely? The immune system exists for a reason — what does it protect against? Fourth, how do biological immune systems distinguish threats from nutrients, and what's the organizational equivalent? Fifth, what's the minimum intervention that changes the immune system's behavior without destroying its protective function?"

**What makes this recipe unique:** It reframes organizational resistance not as dysfunction but as an immune response — so the solution isn't overcoming resistance but educating the immune system. Archetypes Recognition (diagnosing the pattern) + Abductive Cascade (finding structural root) + Absence Counterfactual (understanding the immune system's value) + Biomimicry (importing biological solutions) + Minimum-Intervention Stress Testing (finding the precise lever) is designed for the pattern where the organization systematically kills innovation without anyone intending to.
