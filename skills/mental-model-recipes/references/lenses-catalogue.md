# Lenses Catalogue

Lenses tell you WHAT to notice. They frame reality so that specific patterns become visible. A lens does not tell you what to DO with what you see — that's what operations and moves are for.

Every lens below tells you WHERE TO LOOK. Use operations to decide WHAT TO DO once you look there.

## 1. Distribution & Quantity Patterns

These lenses reveal how things are distributed, proportioned, and scaled.

**Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)**
Directs attention to: asymmetric distributions where a vital few produce most of the results.
→ "Look for the 20% that's driving 80% of the outcome."

**Power Laws**
Directs attention to: extreme distributions where a small number of items dominate massively — more extreme than Pareto. Winner-take-all dynamics.
→ "Look for where one entity has 100x or 1000x the output of the average."

**Normal Distribution (Bell Curve)**
Directs attention to: where most values cluster around a mean with predictable deviation.
→ "Look for where most outcomes are average with symmetric tails."

**Fat Tails (Extremistan)**
Directs attention to: domains where rare events dominate total outcomes and averages are meaningless (Taleb).
→ "Look for where a single event could dwarf all other events combined."

**Diminishing Returns**
Directs attention to: where additional input produces less and less additional output.
→ "Look for where you're past the point of productive investment."

**Critical Mass / Tipping Points**
Directs attention to: thresholds where gradual accumulation suddenly produces dramatic change.
→ "Look for where a small additional push would trigger a cascade."

**Regression to the Mean**
Directs attention to: where extreme results are likely to be followed by more average ones.
→ "Look for where recent performance is probably not a new normal."

**Economies of Scale / Diseconomies of Scale**
Directs attention to: where growth creates advantages (or disadvantages) that compound.
→ "Look for where size itself is creating or destroying value."

## 2. Time & Duration Patterns

These lenses reveal how phenomena behave over time.

**Lindy Effect**
Directs attention to: how long something has survived as a predictor of future survival.
→ "Look for how long this has existed — the longer it's survived, the longer it will likely persist."

**Recency Bias**
Directs attention to: the tendency to overweight recent events and underweight older patterns.
→ "Look for where I'm treating the last data point as the most important one."

**Mean Reversion**
Directs attention to: the tendency for extreme states to return toward the average over time.
→ "Look for where current conditions are unusually extreme and likely to normalize."

**S-Curves**
Directs attention to: the pattern of slow start → rapid growth → plateau that describes adoption, learning, and growth.
→ "Look for where on the S-curve this phenomenon currently sits."

**Hysteresis**
Directs attention to: where a system doesn't return to its original state after the force is removed.
→ "Look for where the damage or change is permanent even if the cause is removed."

**Compounding**
Directs attention to: where small consistent inputs accumulate nonlinearly over time.
→ "Look for where the returns on returns are the real driver."

**Path Dependence**
Directs attention to: where current possibilities are constrained by historical decisions.
→ "Look for where the past has narrowed the available options in ways people aren't tracking."

**Temporal Discounting**
Directs attention to: the tendency to undervalue future outcomes relative to present ones.
→ "Look for where short-term preference is destroying long-term value."

**Shifting Baseline Syndrome**
Directs attention to: how each generation accepts a degraded state as the new normal.
→ "Look for where the benchmark has gradually shifted without anyone noticing."

## 3. Boundaries, Scope & Framing

These lenses reveal where edges, limits, and frames are shaping what's visible.

**Circle of Competence**
Directs attention to: where your knowledge is reliable vs. where you're operating outside your expertise.
→ "Look for whether you're inside or outside your actual zone of competence."

**Map vs. Territory**
Directs attention to: the gap between your representation of reality and reality itself.
→ "Look for where your model of the situation might be distorting the situation."

**Overton Window**
Directs attention to: the range of ideas considered acceptable in current discourse.
→ "Look for what's currently unsayable that might actually be true or useful."

**Frame Control**
Directs attention to: how the way a question or situation is framed determines what answers are possible.
→ "Look for who set the frame — and what becomes visible if you change it."

**Streetlight Effect**
Directs attention to: the tendency to search where it's easy to look rather than where the answer is.
→ "Look for whether you're looking here because it's illuminated rather than because it's relevant."

**McNamara Fallacy**
Directs attention to: the tendency to only consider what can be measured and ignore what can't.
→ "Look for important factors that aren't being measured because they're hard to quantify."

**Availability Heuristic**
Directs attention to: the tendency to overweight whatever comes easily to mind.
→ "Look for whether this seems important because it's important or because it's memorable."

**Sunk Cost Awareness**
Directs attention to: where past investment is distorting current decision-making.
→ "Look for where commitment to a past decision is preventing the right future decision."

## 4. Incentives, Behavior & Agency

These lenses reveal how incentive structures and human nature shape outcomes.

**Goodhart's Law**
Directs attention to: where a measure has become a target and stopped measuring what it was supposed to.
→ "Look for metrics that are being optimized in ways that undermine their original purpose."

**Campbell's Law**
Directs attention to: how high-stakes quantitative indicators corrupt the processes they're intended to monitor (stronger version of Goodhart's).
→ "Look for where the pressure to hit numbers is distorting the system."

**Principal-Agent Problem**
Directs attention to: where the person making decisions has different incentives than the person bearing consequences.
→ "Look for misalignment between who decides and who benefits or suffers."

**Moral Hazard**
Directs attention to: where insulation from consequences encourages risk-taking.
→ "Look for who's taking risks they wouldn't take if they bore the downside."

**Cobra Effect / Perverse Incentives**
Directs attention to: where an intervention creates the opposite of its intended outcome.
→ "Look for where the solution is creating more of the problem."

**Hanlon's Razor**
Directs attention to: the likelihood of incompetence vs. malice as an explanation.
→ "Look for whether stupidity, confusion, or misaligned incentives explain this better than intentional harm."

**Skin in the Game**
Directs attention to: whether decision-makers bear the consequences of their decisions (Taleb).
→ "Look for whether the person recommending this action would do it with their own money/reputation/life."

**Revealed Preferences**
Directs attention to: the gap between what people say they value and what their behavior shows they value.
→ "Look for what actions reveal about true priorities, regardless of stated intentions."

**Mimetic Desire (Girard)**
Directs attention to: how people's wants are shaped by what others want rather than by intrinsic preference.
→ "Look for where desire is being imported from others rather than arising from genuine need."

**Status Games**
Directs attention to: how pursuit of status explains behavior that doesn't make sense through a purely rational lens.
→ "Look for where status competition is the hidden driver."

## 5. Systems & Complexity

These lenses reveal how interconnected elements produce emergent behavior.

**Feedback Loops (Positive and Negative)**
Directs attention to: self-reinforcing (positive) or self-correcting (negative) dynamics.
→ "Look for where the output of a process is feeding back as input, amplifying or dampening the original signal."

**Emergence**
Directs attention to: where system-level properties arise that don't exist in any individual component.
→ "Look for where the whole is doing something none of the parts can do alone."

**Butterfly Effect / Sensitive Dependence**
Directs attention to: where small changes in initial conditions produce vastly different outcomes.
→ "Look for where a tiny input is being amplified into a massive output."

**Leverage Points (Donella Meadows)**
Directs attention to: the specific places in a system where small interventions produce large changes.
→ "Look for where a small push would move the whole system."

**Chesterton's Fence**
Directs attention to: the function of existing structures before proposing their removal.
→ "Look for why this was built before deciding to tear it down."

**Iatrogenics**
Directs attention to: where intervention causes more harm than the original problem.
→ "Look for where doing nothing would produce a better outcome than doing something."

**Antifragility**
Directs attention to: where stressors and volatility make a system stronger rather than weaker (Taleb).
→ "Look for where the system benefits from disorder rather than being harmed by it."

**Gall's Law**
Directs attention to: the principle that complex systems that work always evolve from simple systems that work.
→ "Look for whether you're trying to build something complex from scratch instead of evolving it."

**Homeostasis**
Directs attention to: the tendency of systems to resist change and return to equilibrium.
→ "Look for where the system is actively fighting your intervention."

**Network Effects**
Directs attention to: where each additional user increases the value for all users.
→ "Look for where the connections between nodes are more valuable than the nodes themselves."

**Phase Transitions**
Directs attention to: where gradual changes in conditions suddenly produce a qualitative shift in the system's state.
→ "Look for where the system is about to flip from one state to a fundamentally different one."

## 6. Information & Knowledge

These lenses reveal how information flows, hides, and distorts.

**Asymmetric Information**
Directs attention to: where one party knows significantly more than the other.
→ "Look for who knows what — and what they're not telling you."

**Signal vs. Noise**
Directs attention to: the ratio of meaningful information to random variation.
→ "Look for whether this data point is a real signal or just noise."

**Survivorship Bias**
Directs attention to: the invisible failures that are excluded from the data you're seeing.
→ "Look for who didn't make it — the ones you can't see because they're not here to tell the story."

**Selection Bias**
Directs attention to: distortions in data caused by non-random sampling.
→ "Look for whether the data you're seeing is representative or systematically skewed."

**Base Rate Neglect**
Directs attention to: the tendency to ignore prior probabilities when evaluating specific cases.
→ "Look for how often this type of thing actually happens before evaluating this specific case."

**Narrative Fallacy**
Directs attention to: the human tendency to construct coherent stories from random events (Taleb).
→ "Look for where you're inventing a story to explain what might be coincidence."

**Confirmation Bias**
Directs attention to: the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms existing beliefs.
→ "Look for whether you're searching for evidence or searching for confirmation."

**Berkson's Paradox**
Directs attention to: spurious correlations created by conditioning on a collider variable.
→ "Look for whether the pattern you're seeing is an artifact of how you selected the data."

**Legibility (James C. Scott)**
Directs attention to: how the demand for things to be readable/countable/manageable destroys important complexity.
→ "Look for where the drive to make things legible is flattening essential nuance."

**The Curse of Knowledge**
Directs attention to: the difficulty of imagining what it's like to not know what you know.
→ "Look for where your expertise is blinding you to how others see this."

## 7. Strategy & Competition

These lenses reveal competitive dynamics and strategic positioning.

**Comparative Advantage**
Directs attention to: where you're relatively better, not absolutely better.
→ "Look for what you're least bad at relative to alternatives — not what you're best at in absolute terms."

**Moats / Sustainable Competitive Advantage**
Directs attention to: the structural factors that prevent competition from eroding your position.
→ "Look for what makes this advantage durable rather than temporary."

**Red Queen Effect**
Directs attention to: where you must keep running faster just to stay in the same place.
→ "Look for where the competitive landscape is escalating and standing still means falling behind."

**Mutually Assured Destruction / Nash Equilibrium**
Directs attention to: stable states where no party can improve their position by changing strategy unilaterally.
→ "Look for whether this is a stable equilibrium that no one can break without destroying themselves."

**Asymmetric Warfare**
Directs attention to: where a smaller player can win by changing the rules of the game.
→ "Look for where the rules of engagement favor the incumbent — and what happens if you refuse those rules."

**Winner's Curse**
Directs attention to: where winning a competition means you've overpaid.
→ "Look for whether winning this would actually be losing."

**Barriers to Exit**
Directs attention to: what keeps players in a game they should leave.
→ "Look for what's preventing the rational departure from a bad situation."

**Blue Ocean / Category Creation**
Directs attention to: uncontested market spaces rather than competing in existing ones.
→ "Look for where you can make the competition irrelevant by redefining the game."

## 8. Human Judgment & Decision-Making

These lenses reveal systematic patterns in how humans think and choose.

**Anchoring**
Directs attention to: how initial information disproportionately influences subsequent judgments.
→ "Look for what the first number or frame was — it's probably distorting everything after."

**Loss Aversion**
Directs attention to: the tendency to feel losses roughly 2x more strongly than equivalent gains.
→ "Look for where fear of loss is preventing pursuit of gains."

**Dunning-Kruger Effect**
Directs attention to: the inverse relationship between competence and confidence at low skill levels.
→ "Look for where confidence is highest and calibration is worst."

**Planning Fallacy**
Directs attention to: the systematic tendency to underestimate time, cost, and risk while overestimating benefits.
→ "Look for whether this estimate is based on the inside view (how I hope it goes) or the outside view (how things like this usually go)."

**Hindsight Bias**
Directs attention to: the tendency to see past events as having been predictable.
→ "Look for whether this actually was foreseeable or just seems that way now."

**Paradox of Choice**
Directs attention to: where too many options produces paralysis or reduced satisfaction.
→ "Look for where reducing options would improve decisions."

**Peak-End Rule**
Directs attention to: how people judge experiences primarily by their peak intensity and their ending.
→ "Look for the most intense moment and the final moment — those will dominate the memory."

**Commitment and Consistency Bias**
Directs attention to: the drive to appear consistent with prior statements and actions.
→ "Look for where past commitments are overriding current evidence."

**Halo Effect**
Directs attention to: where a single positive attribute creates an unjustified glow of positive assessment overall.
→ "Look for where expertise or excellence in one area is being incorrectly assumed in another."

## 9. Change, Growth & Evolution

These lenses reveal how things develop, adapt, and transform.

**Natural Selection / Variation + Selection**
Directs attention to: where random variation combined with selection pressure produces adaptation.
→ "Look for what's varying and what's selecting — that's where evolution is happening."

**Punctuated Equilibrium**
Directs attention to: long periods of stability interrupted by sudden rapid change.
→ "Look for whether you're in a stable period or on the edge of a sudden shift."

**Creative Destruction (Schumpeter)**
Directs attention to: how new innovations necessarily destroy old ones.
→ "Look for what this new thing will kill — and what the death creates space for."

**J-Curve**
Directs attention to: where things get worse before they get better after a change.
→ "Look for whether the current deterioration is a sign of failure or the dip before improvement."

**Local Optimum vs. Global Optimum**
Directs attention to: where you're at the best nearby point but not the best overall point.
→ "Look for whether you'd need to get worse before you could get much better."

**Adaptive Valley**
Directs attention to: the fitness landscape where reaching a better state requires crossing through a worse one.
→ "Look for the valley you'd have to cross to reach the higher peak."

**Jevons Paradox**
Directs attention to: where efficiency improvements increase total consumption rather than decrease it.
→ "Look for where making something more efficient is causing more of it to happen."

**Constructive Disruption**
Directs attention to: where breaking existing structures creates the conditions for better ones.
→ "Look for what needs to be broken to unlock the next level."

## 10. Value, Trade-Offs & Allocation

These lenses reveal how value is created, exchanged, and destroyed.

**Opportunity Cost**
Directs attention to: what you're giving up by choosing this option.
→ "Look for what you can't do because you're doing this."

**Marginal Thinking**
Directs attention to: the value of one additional unit rather than the average of all units.
→ "Look for the value of the NEXT unit, not the average."

**Externalities**
Directs attention to: costs or benefits imposed on parties not involved in the transaction.
→ "Look for who's paying costs or receiving benefits that aren't reflected in the price."

**Tragedy of the Commons**
Directs attention to: where individually rational behavior depletes a shared resource.
→ "Look for where everyone is acting rationally and the collective is being destroyed."

**Optionality**
Directs attention to: the value of keeping choices open.
→ "Look for where preserving future flexibility is more valuable than committing now."

**Reversibility**
Directs attention to: whether a decision can be undone.
→ "Look for whether this is a one-way door or a two-way door — and calibrate your caution accordingly."

**Zero-Sum vs. Positive-Sum**
Directs attention to: whether the interaction creates new value or merely redistributes existing value.
→ "Look for whether both parties can win or whether one's gain requires the other's loss."

**Asymmetric Upside / Convexity**
Directs attention to: situations where the potential gain vastly exceeds the potential loss.
→ "Look for where you risk little but stand to gain enormously."
